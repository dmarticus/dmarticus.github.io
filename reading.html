Reading

I consume a lot of content (what can I say, I'm a millennial in 2020) and I like sharing cool stuff I find. So, I figured I'd try and make a running list of links to things I've consumed and why I like them. Call it link curation, call it time-wasting; whatever -- it brings me joy. Perhaps one day I'll make this into a newsletter.

Links (updated weekly)

2020

8/31 - 9/6

  • Typing is Hard. This post does a great job outlining type checking and decidability in an accessible way and provides great links to more involved analysis for the curious reader.
  • Against Online Tooling. The TL;DR is that online tools for things like JWT generation, diff checking, or anything else are access points for accidentally leaking sensitive data. The author encourages doing these operations locally as much as possible.
  • The Bicycle Threat Model. Great introduction to threat modeling via a practical example: keeping your bicycle safe in a city.

8/24 - 8/30

  • Capitalists Or Cronyists. Scott Galloway lays out a theory on why the US is descending into cronyism instead of ascending capitalism.
  • United States Corona Corps. Another Galloway post, this one laying out a radical idea of forming a Peace Corps-like Corona Corps for young people to help with the COVID-19 effort in the US.
  • GPT-3, Bloviator: OpenAI’s language generator has no idea what it’s talking about. This study reports on an analysis GPT-3's ability to reason about complex concepts and observing poor performance. Good contrast to the aggressively-pro GPT-3 marketing FUD.
  • between fked and a hard place. The sad state of higher education's response to COVID-19 in the US.
  • Is WFH affecting the S&P? Yup.
  • Formal methods intro. Lays out useful concepts for understanding Formal Verification literature.
  • But does it help you ship?. Fun read on the actual cost of adding automations.
  • Parser Combinators. Technical deep dive on writing a parse combinator in Rust from scratch.
  • Reachability Modulo Theories and A Solver for Reachability Modulo Theories.
  • The Friendship that made Google Huge. The story of Jeff Dean and Sanjay Ghemawat at Google.
  • Why Efficiency is Dangerous and Slowing Down Makes Life Better. Click bait title aside, this post makes a compelling case for being cautious about aiming for efficiency at all costs.
  • Don't Ask to Ask, Just Ask. This piece speaks to my soul: it's a quick read on a best practice for async communication. Reminds me of nohello.com.
  • Questions. Patrick Collison writes some great posts on the big questions he's chewing on. Inspirational stuff.
  • Empirical Investigation into Programming Language Syntax. A cool read that attempts to answer the question of "which parts programming language syntax is good and which ones are bad?"
  • Insane Story about the Kidnapping of Sultan bin Turki II.
  • Building a Digital Garden. This post inspired me to set up automated processes that show my thinking process and consolidates them in one place.
  • Shadows. Chilling piece on the state of political discourse in the US.
  • Hack Your Career by Troy Hunt. As an aspiring independent consultant this is a great watch
  • Just quit your job at Facebook already.

8/17 - 8/23

  • sled.rs.An incredible read on performance engineering within the context of sled, an open-source DB written in Rust.
  • Steve Yegge's Google Platform rant from 2011. TL;DR Google treats its employees better than its customers and that's why Amazon ate Google's lunch in the cloud platform space.
  • Steve Yegge's Google Deprecation rant from 2020. TL;DR it's more of the same from the above post, just 9 years later.
  • Gabriel Gonzalez's talk on marketing Haskell to mainstream programmers. A bunch of good takeaways from marketing not just Haskell, but any non-mainstream technology.
  • Low-Water Mark (part of a series on design patterns for distributed systems). This post focuses on how to telling logging machinery which portion of the log can be safely discarded to avoid runaway disk storage.
  • Learn in Public. Incredibly inspirational post on blogging, writing, speaking, and embracing the public growth that comes therein.
  • What's the best thing? Fun Tom Scott Project that compares things and shows you how you did against others.
  • Open-sourcing Have I Been Pwned. I loved all of the specifics that Troy Hunt goes into about running a massively successful open-source project.
  • Attack of the Clones: TikTok’s Rival Kuaishou Lands in the US. Ponderous and thorough deep dive of Kuaishou.
  • Open-Source Knowledge. Like the above link about learning in public, the author encourages using blogging, speaking, presenting to open-source your knowledge as well as your tech.
  • Gateway Pages prevent PDF Shock. PDFs on the internet are still a rough experience and the author encourages alternatives to them.
  • On Being a Principal Engineer. Silvia Botros talks about the specifics of succeeding as a senior Individual Contributor.

8/10 - 8/16

  • The Rise of TikTok and Understanding Its Parent Company ByteDance. Deep dive on TikTok. One of the most interesting reads on the company that I've ever encountered.
  • Pinduoduo and Vertically Integrated Social Commerce. How to build a giant.
  • Can you know too much about your organization?. Helps shed some light on why you feel disenchanted about working at the same place for a while despite enjoying your day job.
  • Dorking: How to find anything on the internet. Quick post about query optimization when using Google.
  • Oatly: The New Coke. Ban oat milk on GOD.
  • Drive and Listen. Traveling in the time of travel bans.
  • The Truth Is Paywalled But The Lies Are Free. The title is most of it and the content is kinda fluffy but the implications of this headline are startling.
  • Twitter, George Soros, and Porn (this is my 3rd Margins post in as many weeks, just sub to them already)
  • GaryVee Is Still Preaching the Hustle Gospel in the Middle of a Pandemic. Just trust me.
  • Software disenchantment (an oldie but goodie). There's so much software out there that just isn't done well and there's reason why but it's still sad.

8/3 - 8/9

  • Investing for Designers and Developers. Tech often provides large incomes to younger people who have little experience with making tons of money; this post helps a lot.
  • Cloud Computing Is Not the Energy Hog That Had Been Feared. Positive news about the improvements in energy efficiency and how these improvements benefit cloud computing.
  • CodeGen: Semantic’s improved language support system. Amazing post about applying ASTs to represent code as data and query it as such.
  • The Impact of Toxic Influencers on Communities. Scala has some notorious bad actors in the open-source community and this read is a great overview on how to deal with it. Relevant for any community who has struggled with powerful but dangerous influences.
  • Adventures with Scala Collections. Great read from one of my coworkers on the oft-maligned Scala collections library that makes a case for how effective the library is for most work.
  • Winning the Internet. It's a newsletter about newsletters across the internet! V meta.

7/26 - 8/2

  • The summer job as we know it is over. I mean, we knew this already.
  • How Ferrero Rocher Chocolates Became a Status Symbol for Immigrants.
  • Chuck Norris and Hacking Twitter.
